Andersonville

What's the story behind Andersonville?


Andersonville, a tiny neighborhood on Chicago's far North Side that straddles the border between Edgewater and Uptown, is most known for its vibrant Clark Street strip, its LGBTQ community, and a water tower painted with a Swedish flag that stands atop a museum.

Andersonville originated in the mid-nineteenth century as a tiny settlement of Swedish immigrants, eventually becoming the core of Chicago's Swedish community (which was once second only to Stockholm) in the early 1900s. Swedish-owned bakeries, shoe stores, and delis previously lined Clark Street; now, historic images of some of these companies may be found hanging in the windows of the buildings where they once stood.

Despite the fact that much of the neighborhood's Swedish population relocated to the suburbs in the aftermath of the Great Depression, Andersonville continues to celebrate its Swedish heritage through its Swedish American Museum and annual events such as Midsommarfest and the St. Lucia Festival of Lights. The nabe is currently more recognized for housing one of the city's largest LGBTQ populations, as well as famous restaurants and nightlife spots.


If you only do one thing, make it a good one.


Work up an appetite and stop by Middle East Bakery and Grocery for freshly made pita bread, fatyr (savory hand pies), or baklava. You'll also find dried fruit, almonds, feta cheese, hummus, and olives—all of which are probably less expensive than what your local grocery shop carries.

Off the beaten path
Woolly Mammoth, the neighborhood's premier location for quirky antiques and curiosities, has a collection of taxidermy, vintage medical gear, and creatures floating in formaldehyde.

On a bright day
Take a stroll down Clark Street, stopping at Hopleaf for mussels and a Belgian beer on the terrace, George's Ice Cream and Sweets for a cone, and Rattleback Records for some new music.

On a cloudy day
Before heading to Replay Andersonville to set a few high scores on the numerous pinball tables and arcade games, browse the shelves of Women and Children First and pick up some new reading material.

Learn more about culture.
Attend a presentation of The Infinite Wrench, the Neo-Futurists' hallmark production, to see a sequence of micro-plays. Stop by the Chicago Magic Lounge for an evening performance if you love card tricks, sleight of hand, and baffling illusions.

Take it easy.
Sit down for a drink at Simon's Tavern (order glögg if you're here in the winter) and start up a discussion with some Andersonville natives.

Visit the most recent locations.
On the north end of the neighborhood, there's a new Parson's Chicken and Fish (in the former Stone Fox site), where you can enjoy the same hush puppies and hot chicken offered at the local chain's other locations. If you're looking for a hard drink, Nobody's Darling, a gay, Black women-owned cocktail bar, is an accepting location to sip a Penicillin.

CTA stations closest to you
The Berwyn Red Line station is usually the best option for traveling to Andersonville, but it's presently being reconstructed and won't reopen until 2024. Until then, you can take the Argyle or Thorndale Red Line stations to be dumped off just south or north of Andersonville, depending.
